WHAT IS SEC FORM 13F?

8 min read  ·  InsiderTape

Every quarter, thousands of the world's largest investment managers — hedge funds, mutual funds, pension funds, family offices — are required to tell the SEC exactly what they own. The filing they use is called a Form 13F, and the data inside it is completely public.

This means that every 90 days, you can see exactly what Berkshire Hathaway added to its portfolio. What Citadel held at quarter-end. Which stocks Renaissance Technologies was building positions in. Most retail investors have no idea this data exists, let alone how to use it.

WHO HAS TO FILE A 13F?

The SEC requires any institutional investment manager that exercises discretion over $100 million or more in equity securities to file a 13F quarterly. That threshold captures a huge range of filers:

  • Hedge funds (Bridgewater, Appaloosa, Pershing Square, etc.)
  • Mutual fund companies (Fidelity, T. Rowe Price, etc.)
  • Insurance companies investing premium float
  • Bank trust departments and wealth managers
  • Pension funds and endowments
  • Family offices managing large pools of capital

Passive index funds like Vanguard and BlackRock are included, but their 13Fs are largely routine — they mechanically hold every stock in proportion to the index, so their filings reflect index composition changes more than investment decisions. The more interesting signals come from active managers who are making discretionary bets.

Scale of disclosure: More than 5,000 institutions file 13Fs each quarter. The combined portfolios disclosed represent trillions of dollars in equity holdings — essentially a map of where professional capital is concentrated at any given moment.

WHAT DOES A 13F ACTUALLY CONTAIN?

Each 13F filing includes an information table listing every equity position the manager held at quarter-end. For each position, the filing discloses:

Field What it means
Issuer Name The company whose shares are held. Identified by CUSIP (a unique security identifier), not ticker symbol — which is why CUSIP-to-ticker mapping is required to make the data usable.
Shares / Principal Amount Number of shares held at quarter-end. This is the key number — compare it against the prior quarter to see whether the position grew, shrank, or was new.
Market Value Dollar value of the position at quarter-end, reported in thousands. A $10M position appears as "10,000" in the filing.
Investment Discretion Whether the manager has sole discretion over the position (SOLE), shared discretion (SHARED), or is advising without discretion (DFND). Sole discretion is the most meaningful signal — that's a real active bet.
Put / Call If the position is in options (puts or calls) rather than shares, this field flags it. Pure equity positions have no entry here.

Crucially, the 13F does not disclose the price at which shares were acquired, when during the quarter the position was built, or what the manager's thesis is. You see the destination, not the journey.

THE FILING TIMELINE

13Fs are due within 45 days after the end of each calendar quarter. That means:

  • Q1 (Jan–Mar) filings due by May 15
  • Q2 (Apr–Jun) filings due by August 14
  • Q3 (Jul–Sep) filings due by November 14
  • Q4 (Oct–Dec) filings due by February 14

The lag is important to understand. When Q4 13F data drops in mid-February, the positions it describes were as of December 31. A fund could have added a stock in October, held it through year-end for the 13F snapshot, and sold it entirely in January — and you'd never know. The 13F shows you what was held; it doesn't tell you what's being held right now.

The staleness problem: By the time you see a 13F, the information is 6-10 weeks old. Treat it as evidence of sustained conviction — a position held for a full quarter — rather than a live signal. The longer a manager has held a position across multiple quarters, the more meaningful it becomes.

HOW TO READ THE SIGNAL IN 13F DATA

The raw filing is a list of holdings. The signal is in the change between filings. To extract it, you need to compare the current quarter's 13F against the prior quarter for the same manager:

New positions (most interesting)

A stock that appears in the current 13F but wasn't in last quarter's filing is a new position. An analyst team did enough work to build conviction from scratch, pushed it through an investment committee, and allocated capital. That process typically takes weeks or months. New positions from funds with strong track records are the 13F equivalent of an insider making their first open-market purchase.

Additions (confirming)

The manager held the stock last quarter and added more shares this quarter. Their thesis is playing out, or they want more exposure at current prices. Large additions (more than 25% increase in position size) signal meaningful new conviction, not just drift.

Reductions (worth watching)

The manager cut their stake but didn't exit entirely. This could mean profit-taking, risk management, or softening conviction. A single reduction is ambiguous. The same stock being reduced by multiple unrelated funds in the same quarter is a clearer signal of institutional consensus shifting.

Exits (potentially significant)

The position drops to zero. The manager held it last quarter and doesn't hold it this quarter. If the exit comes after a long period of holding (multiple quarters), it means something changed in their analysis. Multiple simultaneous exits from previously committed holders is a strong signal worth tracking.

Convergence is the real signal: Any individual fund's decision could be idiosyncratic — a fund flow need, a portfolio rebalancing, a bet size limit. But when multiple unrelated funds make the same decision in the same quarter independently, that convergence is hard to explain away. It suggests the investment case is widely understood and agreed upon across professional analysts.

WHAT 13F DATA DOESN'T TELL YOU

Being precise about the limits is as important as understanding the signal.

  • No cost basis. You don't know what the fund paid. A position with a large unrealized gain may be held purely for tax reasons, not because the fund still has conviction.
  • No short positions. The 13F only covers long equity positions. Hedge funds that are simultaneously short a stock don't disclose that. A fund's long 13F position could be a hedge against a larger short in the same company or sector.
  • No derivatives detail beyond puts/calls. Swaps, forwards, and other equity-linked instruments may not be captured.
  • Stale by definition. As described above, you're always looking backward. The position may no longer exist.

InsiderTape processes 13F filings from thousands of institutions each quarter, compares them against the prior quarter's holdings, and surfaces only the changes — new positions, meaningful additions, reductions, and exits. Flat positions that didn't change are filtered out entirely.
